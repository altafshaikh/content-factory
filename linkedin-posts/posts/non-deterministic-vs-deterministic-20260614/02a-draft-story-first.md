I built an automation with AI agents that worked perfectly on the first run.

Same input. Perfect output. I was excited.

Then I ran it again a week later. It skipped two instructions. The output was off.

I added more rules to the agent. It worked again. Then a month later, the AI company pushed an update to the underlying model. Suddenly my automation was making mistakes it had never made before.

I hadn't changed a single line.

Here's what I've been thinking about since then.

When you write code in Python or JavaScript, you get a guarantee: same input, same output. Every time. Forever. Until you change the code.

With AI agents, you get probabilities. The agent might follow all your instructions today. It might skip one tomorrow. The model that powers it might get updated by the provider — quietly, without your input — and your carefully tuned automation starts drifting.

You never fully own an AI agent the way you own a function you wrote.

The real cost isn't building the agent. It's maintaining it. It's reading the outputs every run to catch the drift. It's adding new guardrails when the model changes. It's knowing that you can never fully forget about it.

Programmatic automation: write it once, test it, deploy it, move on.

Agent-based automation: write it, test it, deploy it, monitor it, update it, monitor it again.

I'm not saying agents aren't useful — I use them every day and the output quality is real. But I think we're underselling the maintenance burden to people who are just getting started.

The AI companies are promising one thing. The on-ground experience as a builder is different.

What's your approach to this? Have you found a way to make AI automations more stable, or do you treat them as living systems?
